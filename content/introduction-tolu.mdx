<p>
  Tolu pays ₦4,500 a month for an online course platform she's three modules

</p>
<p>

  subscription is gone until she tries to log in three days later and finds
  herself locked out of a course she was two modules from finishing.
</p>
<p>
  She didn't cancel. Nobody told her anything was wrong. The subscription just
  stopped, because a charge failed on the wrong day and nothing was built to
  notice.
</p>

<h2 id="h-adaeze">And there is Adaeze</h2>
<p>
  Adaeze runs the platform Tolu subscribes to. Forty-something paying customers,
  growing every month, and until recently she was tracking who'd paid, who'd
  lapsed, and who needed a nudge in a spreadsheet she updated by hand every few
  days.
</p>
<p>
  She isn't bad at this. She built the whole platform herself. But recurring
  billing isn't the product she set out to build, it's the tax she pays for
  having a product worth paying for monthly. Every failed card is revenue that
  just quietly stops arriving, and by the time she notices a customer has
  lapsed, it's often too late to win them back with anything better than a cold
  "hey, did you mean to cancel?" email.
</p>
<p>Nomba Subscription Engine is for both of them.</p>

<h2 id="h-what-it-is">What Nomba Subscription Engine is</h2>
<p>
  Nomba Subscription Engine gives Adaeze a billing layer that doesn't lose Tolu
  over a bad-timing decline.
</p>
<p>
  Adaeze creates a plan through the API, ₦4,500/month, seven-day trial. Tolu
  subscribes through Adaeze's checkout flow; her card is tokenised once through
  Nomba Checkout and stored for reuse. Every month, the engine attempts the
  charge automatically against that stored card.
</p>
<p>
  On the 25th, the charge fails. Instead of silently dropping the subscription,
  the engine moves it to <code>past_due</code> and opens a grace period. A
  WhatsApp message goes to Tolu the same hour:{" "}
  <em>
    "Your payment for [Adaeze's platform] didn't go through. Retry Now / Pause /
    Downgrade."
  </em>{" "}
  Three days later, payday behind her, Tolu replies <strong>Retry Now</strong>.
  The charge succeeds. The subscription returns to <code>active</code>, no gap,
  no re-signup, no lost course progress.
</p>
<p>
  Every transition (the failed charge, the grace period, the WhatsApp exchange,
  the successful retry) is written to an append-only event store. A webhook
  fires to Adaeze's system the moment the subscription recovers, so her platform
  re-grants access automatically, with no manual check on her end.
</p>
<p>
  Adaeze opens her dashboard that evening and sees it in the numbers: one
  past-due subscription, recovered via WhatsApp, MRR unaffected.
</p>
<p>That is Nomba Subscription Engine.</p>

<h2 id="h-underneath">What is underneath</h2>
<p>
  Every payment runs through the <strong>Nomba API</strong>, Checkout for
  tokenisation, the Charge API for each billing-cycle attempt, Transfers where a
  merchant's fee structure requires payout handling, and inbound Nomba webhooks
  that trigger internal state transitions the moment a payment event lands.
</p>
<p>
  The service itself is a NestJS API on PostgreSQL, with BullMQ and Redis
  running recovery and webhook-delivery jobs asynchronously so a slow WhatsApp
  provider or a flaky downstream endpoint never blocks a request. JWT access and
  refresh tokens authenticate merchant users; every business resource is scoped
  to a <code>merchantId</code> at the data-access layer, not just the
  application layer.
</p>
<p>
  Recovery reaches subscribers on whichever channel keeps working when the
  others don't:
</p>

    </div>
  </a>
  <a className="card" href="/channels/ussd">
    <div className="card-icon">☎️</div>

    <div className="card-desc">

      check subscription status, pause, or cancel, built behind an interface
      that can run against a real short-code provider or a simulator, so the
      recovery workflow isn't blocked on a telco integration timeline.
    </div>
  </a>

  three systems slowly drifting out of sync with each other.
</p>
